Where You Win & Where You Don't

Your Advantages Over Everyone

  • Only one with direct AOD measurement. SunsetWx infers atmospheric clarity from moisture. You actually measure aerosol optical depth via Open-Meteo. This is a genuine technical edge — AOD is the physics-based gold standard for light scattering.
  • Full scoring transparency. Every competitor is a black box. You show the exact breakdown — cloud 34/35, humidity 21/25, AOD +4, etc. Users (and you) can debug why a prediction was right or wrong.
  • Sunrise-specific calibration. Everyone else uses the same model for sunrise AND sunset. Your humidity curve is shifted for dawn RH, your cloud optimal range accounts for sunrise light geometry. This matters — dawn and dusk have different atmospheric conditions.
  • Seasonal solar angle. Nobody else adjusts for the fact that winter sunrises are inherently more vivid (lower angle = longer atmospheric path = more Rayleigh scattering). Your ±2 seasonal bonus is scientifically correct and unique.
  • Temporal post-rain detection. Competitors can't distinguish "it rained last night and cleared" from "it hasn't rained in weeks." Your Night.HoursOfRain signal catches the aerosol-washout conditions that produce the best sunrises.
  • AI photography insights. No competitor generates contextual tips about what to shoot, where to stand, or how the conditions will affect photos. Your Groq integration adds real user value beyond a number.
  • $2/month total operating cost. SunsetWx is B2B-priced. SkyCandy relies on multiple paid APIs. You run the core on a $2 AccuWeather plan + free Open-Meteo.

Where SunsetWx Still Beats You

  • Multi-level cloud analysis. This is the big one. SunsetWx ingests cloud data at every tropospheric level (surface to 200mb) and weights high clouds the most. You only have the ceiling height (base of lowest cloud layer). You can't see high cirrus above a low stratus deck — and that high cirrus is what catches pre-sunrise color.
  • Pressure tendency. SunsetWx's 2nd-most-weighted variable. Falling pressure → frontal passages → that dramatic "clearing storm" look. AccuWeather's $2 plan doesn't expose barometric trend, so you can't match this.
  • Raw model resolution. They ingest direct 4km NAM output. You're one abstraction layer away, getting AccuWeather's processed interpretation of weather models. Some nuance is lost in translation.
  • Global coverage. SunsetWx covers the entire planet via GFS. You're Chennai-only. Not a flaw in your algorithm, but a scope limitation.
  • Sunset prediction. You only do sunrise. They do both. Again, scope not algorithm quality.
  • Forecast range. They forecast 4 days out. You're limited to ~12 hours (AccuWeather 12-hour hourly). Users who plan ahead can't use your tool for trip planning.
  • Field-validated accuracy. SunsetWx has years of anecdotal validation + Stanford's independent AUC study. Your 78% is self-assessed against meteorological principles, not against actual sunrise observations from the field.

The Bottom Line

For a $2/month single-developer project, Seaside Beacon v3 is punching well above its weight. You're #2 in algorithm sophistication — behind a VC-funded Penn State meteorology team that partners with The Weather Channel, and ahead of the consumer apps that just reskin SunsetWx's API.

Your two unique technical edges (direct AOD measurement and seasonal solar angle) are things even SunsetWx doesn't have. Your transparency (full scoring breakdown) is something no competitor offers. And your Chennai-specific dawn calibration makes you more accurate for your target market than any global tool could be.

The gap to #1 is primarily about data access: multi-level cloud profiles and pressure tendency require either raw NWP model output or a higher-tier API plan. If you ever upgrade to AccuWeather's premium tier or integrate GFS data directly, that gap closes significantly.
